The Catholic Church recognises the validity of the sacrament of confirmation as conferred by most Eastern non-Catholic Churches (see canons 882 and 845). The second sponsor (godparent) is optional. If he or she is Catholic, he or she must have received all three sacraments of initiation and be actively living the faith. If he or she is a baptised Christian, but not a Catholic, he or she may serve as a Christian witness. Ascription

Canons 111 and 112. See also CCEO canon 35. Note that a person, once ascribed to a Church, cannot cease to be ascribed. For example, someone who was baptised Eastern Orthodox but now considers himself to be Protestant is still considered to be ascribed to the Eastern Orthodox Church in which he was baptised. Similarly, someone who has once been ascribed to any Catholic Church sui iuris can never change ascription to a non-Catholic Church.
Canon 112 §1 3°. Children under the age of fourteen automatically change ascription following their parents. This is generally held to apply also to children whose parents come into the full communion of the Catholic Church.

A member of an Eastern non-Catholic Church, when received into the full communion of the Catholic Church, is ascribed to the corresponding Eastern Catholic Church sui iuris.

This is most likely the Latin Catholic Church.

Canon 910.

Holy Communion

  • Any cleric has the faculty to administer Holy Communion.
  • An extraordinary minister might be deputed to administer Holy Communion, in accord with the norm of law.
Canons 852 §1, 866, 883 2°, and 885 §2. Those over the age of seven who receive baptism are also to be confirmed and to make their first Holy Communion in the same liturgy. Adults received into full communion are also to be confirmed and to make their first Holy Communion in the same liturgy. Canon 852 only applies its definition of adult to those who receive baptism, so children received into full communion may be confirmed in the same liturgy or at a later time.
Canon 112 §1 3°. Children under the age of fourteen whose parents enter into full communion are generally held to automatically enter into full communion together with their parents.
Canon 914. Any Catholic over the age of reason (about seven) should receive Holy Communion regularly.

Summary

Based on the current information, the ceremony should proceed in this order:

  1. Reception into full communion (if pastorally appropriate)
  2. Baptism
  3. Confirmation (if pastorally appropriate)
  4. First Holy Communion

A deacon will not be able to be the celebrant of these rites.

The law is unclear about whether a child between the ages of seven and eighteen should be received into full communion through a formal rite. If the child's parents have been received into full communion after the baptism of the child and before the child's fourteenth birthday, then it is commonly held that the child has been received into full communion by his or her parents' act. If the child's parents are not Catholic, careful pastoral discernment is needed.
The confirmation of a child received into full communion may be delayed to a later time, if appropriate.

Based on the above information, no additional rites are needed at this time.
